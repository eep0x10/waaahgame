Abilities & Keywords

GHB 2025-26 · April 2026 Battlescroll Active

Every unit in Age of Sigmar has abilities on its warscroll, and every ability carries one or more type keywords that govern when and how it can be used. Understanding these types is fundamental to reading warscrolls correctly.

Passive Abilities

An ability tagged [Passive] is always in effect — it does not require activation, spending Command Points, or any deliberate choice to trigger. Passive abilities are simply true whenever their condition is met. Examples include permanent auras that affect nearby friendly units, bonuses that apply during specific phases, and modifiers conditional on unit size or terrain position.

Example. “While this unit is within 12” of a friendly HERO, add 1 to the Hit rolls of this unit’s attacks.” — This is a Passive ability. No activation is required; it simply applies automatically whenever the condition (proximity to a HERO) is met.

Used Abilities

A [Used] ability (sometimes called an Active ability) must be deliberately activated during a specific phase of the turn. The warscroll specifies exactly when it can be used — for example, “Once Per Battle, in your Hero Phase.” Using a Used ability typically does not cost Command Points unless the ability explicitly states it does. Once-per-battle or once-per-round limits are tracked on the warscroll.

Reaction Abilities

A [Reaction] ability is triggered in direct response to an opponent’s action. Reactions are used out of sequence — the opponent takes an action (such as completing a charge or beginning their Hero Phase), and you may then respond by using a qualifying Reaction ability. Reactions cannot be pre-emptively declared; they must respond to a specific, just-occurred trigger. A single trigger can only be responded to once per ability per player.

Ward Saves

A Ward save is an additional defensive roll made after damage has been determined — it is not part of the standard Save roll. The sequence for an attack that inflicts damage is:

  1. Roll to Hit (attacker)
  2. Roll to Wound (attacker)
  3. Roll to Save (defender, using Save characteristic ± Rend)
  4. Apply Damage to the unit’s Health pool
  5. Roll Ward (defender) — each point of damage may be negated on a successful Ward roll

Ward saves are typically expressed as a target number (e.g. “Ward 5+” means roll a D6 per point of damage; on a 5 or 6 that damage is negated). Ward saves are not the same as the standard Save — they occur afterwards and can protect against damage that already passed the Save. Crucially, Ward saves can also protect against Mortal Wounds that bypassed the Save entirely.

Important. Multiple Ward saves do not stack on the same source of damage. If a model would have a 5+ Ward from two different sources, it still only rolls once per damage point. The better Ward (lower target) takes precedence.

Rend

Rend is a weapon characteristic that makes it harder for the target to save against that attack. A weapon’s Rend value is subtracted from the target’s Save characteristic for that attack.

For example: a unit with a Save of 4+ targeted by a weapon with Rend 1 must now roll a 5+ to save. Rend 2 against a 4+ Save requires a 6+; Rend 3 makes the save impossible (a roll of 7+ is required, which no D6 can achieve — the save automatically fails unless a Ward intervenes).

Rend is not a separate “armour penetration” pool — it is a direct modifier to the opponent’s Save roll for that specific attack. Positive modifiers to Save (such as cover bonuses) can offset Rend.

Mortal Wounds

Mortal Wounds are a special category of damage that bypasses the standard attack sequence entirely. When an ability deals Mortal Wounds, you do not roll to Hit, roll to Wound, or give the target a Save roll. The wounds are applied directly to the target’s Health pool — one Mortal Wound equals one point of damage.

Mortal Wounds are not completely unstoppable, however. Ward saves still apply against Mortal Wounds. A unit with a 5+ Ward can attempt to negate each Mortal Wound dealt to it, regardless of whether it was caused by a normal attack or a Mortal Wound ability.

Common sources of Mortal Wounds: spell miscast damage, prayer critical effects, certain weapon critical hit bonuses (on a roll of 6 to Hit/Wound), and dedicated Mortal Wound abilities on warscrolls and faction rules.

Companion Units

The Companion Units keyword on a Hero’s warscroll lists which unit types may be included in that Hero’s Regiment. When building a Regiment (see Army Composition), you select a Hero as the Regiment’s leader, then add up to three additional units. Those units must appear on the Hero’s Companion Units list — a Hero that lacks a matching keyword for a unit cannot take that unit in their Regiment (though the unit could still appear as an Auxiliary).

Companion Units are a deliberate restriction on combined-arms army building: not all Heroes can lead all units, and the faction design guides which archetypes work together within a single Regiment.

Manifestations

Manifestations are summonable battlefield effects that occupy a physical footprint on the table. They are distinct from both regular units and spells — a Manifestation is not a unit (it cannot be targeted by abilities that affect units unless specifically noted), nor is it simply a spell effect. Manifestations persist on the battlefield until dismissed or destroyed.

Manifestations are drawn from Manifestation Lores, which each faction accesses through their Wizards or Priests. Each Wizard or Priest may attempt to summon one Manifestation per Hero Phase. Manifestations have their own profiles — including Health — and can be removed by opponent abilities or when the summoning Wizard is destroyed. Only one copy of any given Manifestation may be in play at a time.

Manifestations vs. Spells. Summoning a Manifestation follows the spellcasting process (roll 2D6 vs. casting value, can be unbound) but the Manifestation itself is not a spell effect and does not expire at the end of the turn. Think of Manifestations as long-lived conjurations that must be physically dealt with.
